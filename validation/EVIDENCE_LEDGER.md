## G4 headline — strong-baseline floor (the false-floor test applied reflexively)

The paper's verdict is a **negative** ("affect encoders match the amygdala single-neuron geometry no better than
generic object/low-level encoders"). By the house central law, a negative inherits the weakness of its
strongest-**failing** baseline, so we re-ran the anchor RSA with paradigmatically-different **stronger** encoders on
both sides: generic — DINOv2-base (self-distilled) + CLIP ViT-B/32 (contrastive); affect — a modern facial-expression
ViT. Per-encoder match to the 442-unit amygdala geometry: **DINOv2 +0.592** (≈ ViT, > both affect encoders), CLIP
+0.374, FER-ViT (affect) **+0.140** (the modern affect probe matched *worse*, not better). The affect(best)−generic(best)
negative **holds across all four configurations and strengthens** under stronger generics: original −0.086 (p=.033) →
+strong-generic −0.088 (p=.014) → +strong-affect −0.084 (p=.039) → +both −0.091 (p=.026). Conclusion: the negative is
**not** a weak-affect-encoder artifact; it survives the representation-invariance red-team the paper itself prescribes.
